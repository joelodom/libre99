## Prologue: Friday, October 28, 1983

The quarterly earnings release that Texas Instruments issued that Friday morning ran to only a few paragraphs, and most of them were routine. Buried in the middle was a sentence that ended an era: the company was withdrawing from the home computer business. The write-off attached to the decision was reported at roughly a third of a billion dollars — at the time, one of the largest sums any American corporation had ever paid to stop doing something.

In Lubbock, Texas, where the machines were designed and built, the announcement landed on a workforce that had spent four years assembling what TI's advertising called *the first 16-bit home computer* — a silver-and-black console with a real metal trim strip, a cartridge slot on the right, and, if you bought the sidecar, a voice. Thousands of people in a mid-sized West Texas city built computers for a living on Thursday and, in the way these things go, spent Friday learning what "discontinued" would mean for them.

Here is the strange part, the part that makes this machine worth a thousand-page book: the TI-99/4A did not stop selling. It sped up. TI cut the console loose at clearance prices — $49.95 was a common sticker that Christmas — and Americans who had watched two years of brutal price-war advertising finally pounced. In the last weeks of 1983, a discontinued computer was one of the hottest gifts in the country. Warehouses emptied. By most accounts, somewhere near 2.8 million consoles had been made by the time the lines stopped, and a large share of them found homes *after* the machine was officially dead.

Which means the TI-99/4A entered 1984 with something almost no orphaned computer ever gets: an enormous installed base, a suddenly ex-corporate community with nothing to lose, and a fully documented assembly-language development system already on store shelves. The company left. The machine stayed. User groups swelled instead of dissolving; newsletters and magazines sprang up to replace the official ones; third-party engineers began building the peripherals TI never would. Four decades later the community is still shipping new hardware, new tools, and new software of genuinely commercial polish — and this book exists because that living platform is, as we will argue all the way to Chapter 45, one of the best classrooms ever built for learning how computers actually work.

But to program this machine well, you need to know how it came to be — because nearly every strange thing about it, every constraint you will wrestle with from Chapter 4 onward, was put there by a decision someone made in a conference room between 1976 and 1981. This chapter is the story of those decisions, the war they led to, and the community that outlived them.

## The Bridge: Why a Programmer Needs This History

On a modern machine, you program against abstractions chosen by committees you will never meet — instruction sets, ABIs, driver models — and the history behind them almost never leaks into your code. On the TI-99/4A the leakage is total. When you ask, in Chapter 5, why a 16-bit CPU sees most of its memory through an 8-bit funnel, the answer is not an engineering principle; it is a cancelled chip and a shipping deadline. When you ask, in Chapter 25, why cartridge software lives in a bizarre serially-addressed memory that no other computer ever used, the answer is a royalty strategy. When you ask why there are only 256 bytes of fast RAM, why the operating system is written in an interpreted bytecode, why the machine speaks — every answer is in this chapter.

Think of the history as the machine's *requirements document*, recovered after the fact. Modern codebases rot when nobody remembers why a module exists; this platform is the same phenomenon at hardware scale, except that the community spent forty years doing the archaeology, and we get to inherit their findings. Read this chapter once for the story. You will find yourself returning to it from the technical chapters, because on this machine, *why* and *how* are the same question asked in different decades.

## 1.1 Two Bloodlines: Texas Instruments Before the Home Computer

Texas Instruments did not begin as an electronics company. It began, in 1930, as Geophysical Service Incorporated — an oil-exploration outfit that used reflection seismography to find petroleum under the Texas dirt. Signal processing was the family trade from the very first day; the company took the name Texas Instruments in 1951, just in time for the technology that would define it.

That technology arrived in a hurry. In 1954, TI's Gordon Teal — hired away from Bell Labs — announced the first commercial *silicon* transistors, leapfrogging the fragile germanium devices of the day, and that same year TI supplied the transistors inside the Regency TR-1, the first mass-market transistor radio. Four years later, in September 1958, a new TI engineer named Jack Kilby, working through the summer shutdown because he hadn't yet accrued vacation, demonstrated a circuit whose components were formed in a single piece of semiconductor. The integrated circuit — for which Kilby would eventually share a Nobel Prize — made TI one of the two or three companies that could plausibly claim to have invented the modern world.

But inventing components and selling them to other manufacturers is a low-glamour business, and TI's leadership spent the 1960s and 70s pushing a doctrine of *vertical integration*: don't just sell the chip, sell the product built around the chip. The proof of concept was the handheld calculator. In 1967 Kilby, Jerry Merryman, and James Van Tassel built the prototype (project name "Cal Tech") precisely to create a consumer market for TI's own circuits; by 1972 the Datamath TI-2500 was on shelves, and within a few years TI had fought and won a savage calculator price war against a field of competitors who bought their chips — often from TI — at prices TI itself did not have to pay.

The calculator war matters to our story because of *how* TI won it. The company was famous for what business schools call experience-curve pricing: set the price below today's cost, because volume will drive tomorrow's cost lower still, and competitors who can't ride the curve will bleed out first. It worked in calculators. It became something close to corporate religion. It would be tried again in home computers, against an opponent for whom it did not work at all — but that is §1.6's story.

Two more pieces complete the first bloodline. TI's consumer empire was run largely out of Lubbock, Texas, where calculators and watches were designed and built; the watch business, incidentally, ended badly in a price collapse around the turn of the decade, a warning shot nobody chose to hear. And in 1978, out of that same consumer group, came the product that gave the 99/4 its voice: **Speak & Spell**. Conceived by Paul Breedlove as a spelling tutor that could *say* the words, and built by a team including Richard Wiggins, Gene Frantz, and Larry Brantingham, it squeezed human speech through a technique called linear predictive coding into a single-chip synthesizer at a toy's price point. Nobody else in consumer electronics could do it. Speech became TI's signature move — and when the home computer needed a differentiator, the speech chip was waiting (Ch. 20 is devoted to it).

The second bloodline ran through Houston and Austin rather than Lubbock: TI's *computer* business. Through the 1970s TI built a respectable line of 16-bit minicomputers, the TI-990 family, machines that sold into industrial control, data collection, and terminals. The 990 line had an unusual and rather elegant central idea — its registers lived in main memory rather than on the processor — and in 1976 TI did something genuinely bold with it: it compressed a 990-class processor onto a single chip and called it the **TMS9900**. The two bloodlines — a consumer division that knew how to make silicon talk and sell for $50, and a computer division with a real 16-bit CPU on one die — existed in the same corporation, mostly ignoring each other. The TI-99/4 is what happened when management pushed them together.

## 1.2 The 9900 Gamble

Hold the TMS9900's birthdate in mind: 1976. Intel's 8086 was two years away; Motorola's 68000, three. When the 9900 shipped, you could buy a genuine 16-bit minicomputer architecture — 16-bit words, 16-bit ALU, a 64 K byte address space, hardware multiply and divide — in a single package, from a top-tier manufacturer, years before anyone else offered such a thing. On paper, TI had lapped the field.

The architecture itself was the 990's, and it was strange and beautiful. The 9900 has almost no registers on the chip. Instead, a **workspace pointer** designates a 32-byte region of ordinary RAM, and the sixteen 16-bit "registers" R0–R15 are simply that region's contents. Switching contexts — for an interrupt, or a subroutine package — means loading three hardware registers (workspace pointer, program counter, status), not saving sixteen; it is a minicomputer's idea of elegance, and in Chapter 4 you will come to appreciate how much design leverage it gives you. You will also discover its price: every register operation is, physically, a memory operation. On a machine with fast RAM everywhere, that is a fair trade. Remember that clause — *with fast RAM everywhere* — because the 99/4's designers were about to violate it.

Why didn't the world's head-start 16-bit micro conquer the world? Partly packaging and cost: the 9900 came in a huge 64-pin package, wanted three supply voltages and an external four-phase clock, and priced accordingly. Partly the address space: 64 K bytes with no segmentation or banking on the chip looked roomy in 1976 and claustrophobic by 1980, exactly when the 8086's segmented megabyte and the 68000's flat sixteen megabytes arrived. And partly it was TI's own inward gaze. In the doctrine of vertical integration, microprocessors existed substantially to power TI's *own* systems; evangelizing outside designers, seeding second sources, and lavishing support on strangers — the things Intel and Motorola did obsessively — were never the priority. The 9900's cut-down siblings told the same story: the 8-bit-bus TMS9980 was slow and unloved, and the one derivative that might have mattered most, a chip called the TMS9985, never made it out of the building at all.

That cancelled chip is the hinge of this entire book, and it belongs to the next section.

## 1.3 Building the 99/4 (1977–1979): Design by Committee, Rescue by Bodge

The home computer project took shape in the late 1970s inside the Lubbock consumer group — the calculator and Speak & Spell people — under intense senior-management interest. That interest was not gentle. Contemporary accounts and later recollections from participants agree on the flavor: a project reviewed often and steered from far above, by executives whose formative wins had been calculators, aimed at a market nobody yet understood. Requirements accreted. The machine had to be friendly enough for a family, closed enough to protect TI's software revenue, cheap enough to hit a consumer price, and impressive enough to justify the corporate slogan-in-waiting: the first 16-bit home computer.

The plan, originally, did not involve the full TMS9900 at all. The consumer machine was designed around a forthcoming cost-reduced derivative — the TMS9985, a 9900-family core with an 8-bit external bus, intended to be cheap the way consumer silicon must be. The system architecture assumed it: an 8-bit-wide world of memory and peripherals. Then the 9985 died in development. With the machine's schedule and silicon already committed, the team performed the rescue that defines the platform: they took the full 16-bit TMS9900 and grafted it onto the 8-bit board through a multiplexer, splitting every 16-bit bus transaction into two 8-bit halves with wait states — for *almost* everything. One small island was left genuinely 16 bits wide: 256 bytes of static RAM wired directly to the CPU, known forever after as the **scratchpad**. If you want to know why Chapter 24 lavishes sixteen pages on a quarter kilobyte, and why Chapter 37's first commandment of optimization is *placement*, this paragraph is why. The fastest memory on the machine is fast because it predates the bodge; everything else pays the multiplexer's toll. (We will measure that toll with a stopwatch in Chapter 5.)

The rest of the 99/4 assembled around that rescue. For video, TI's semiconductor group supplied what became one of the era's great chips: the TMS9918 Video Display Processor, with its own 16 K of private DRAM, generating a 256×192 display of characters and — a genuine novelty in 1979 — hardware **sprites** (Part III). For sound, a three-voice-plus-noise generator (Ch. 19). For software, the machine carried a full-screen editor of a BASIC in ROM — except that it wasn't quite in ROM, and it wasn't quite the machine's native tongue, for reasons the next section explains.

Two external forces then shaped the product customers actually saw. The first was the keyboard: to save cost and preserve a consumer-appliance feel, the 99/4 shipped with calculator-style keys — a small array of stiff rectangular buttons, no lowercase, arranged in a layout only approximately QWERTY. Reviewers were merciless, and they were right. The second was the United States government. Under the FCC's radio-interference rules as they stood while the 99/4 was being finished, TI could not get approval to sell the machine with the usual switch-box RF modulator that let a home computer use a television as its display. Rather than wait (the rules were loosened soon after — a bitter detail), TI bundled its own display: a rebadged 13-inch Zenith color monitor, sold with the console as a package. The pairing pushed the June 1979 price to **$1,150** — Apple II territory, for a machine with chiclet keys and a locked cartridge door. It reached stores in the fall of 1979 and landed with a thud. Sales figures from the period are murky, but no one, then or now, has called the 99/4 anything but a failure; the kindest reading is that it was an expensive public beta.

> **Sidebar — The Lubbock Plant.** It is worth pausing on how unusual TI's consumer geography was. The industry's other home computers came from Silicon Valley, suburban Philadelphia, Tokyo. TI's came from Lubbock, a flat, windy college-and-cotton city in West Texas where the company had planted its consumer division — calculators, watches, learning aids, and then computers — far from both Dallas headquarters and any other electronics culture. When the 99/4A boomed, Lubbock hired in the thousands; local memory of the era is of overtime, of neighbors on the console line, of a company town within a town. When Black Friday came, it came to Lubbock hardest. The machines in this book carry a "Texas Instruments" badge, but the people who built them would tell you the address mattered: this was the *Lubbock* computer.

## 1.4 The Closed Box: GROM as a Business Strategy

Every home computer maker of 1979 faced the same strategic question: once the console is sold, who profits from the software? TI's answer was the most aggressive in the industry, and it was implemented not in a contract but in silicon.

TI invented a proprietary memory device called the **GROM** — Graphics Read-Only Memory. A GROM is a ROM that hides its contents behind a deliberately odd interface: instead of sitting in the CPU's address space like honest memory, it holds an internal address counter and dribbles its bytes out serially through a pair of I/O ports (Ch. 25 dissects the protocol down to the off-by-one). Only TI fabricated GROMs. And the console's cartridge system — the "Solid State Software™ Command Modules" — was built around them, right down to the operating system's menu, which politely enumerates whatever GROM-resident software it finds at power-on (Ch. 28 walks that code).

Pair this with the machine's second secret: most TI software, including the console's own operating system and TI BASIC itself, was not written in TMS9900 machine code. It was written in **GPL** — Graphics Programming Language — a compact bytecode executed by an interpreter in the console ROM, fetching its instructions out of GROM (Part VI teaches you to write it). GPL existed for defensible engineering reasons: bytecode is dense, GROMs were cheap for TI to make, and the language abstracted a hardware family TI planned to build. But it was also a moat. A third-party developer in 1980 faced a language with no public manual, a memory device no one else could manufacture, and a company whose stated intention was that outside software would be published *through TI*, on TI's terms, with TI's margin.

The strategy had exactly the two effects you would predict. First, the software library stayed small and overwhelmingly first-party — respectable in education (TI's partnerships put real curriculum on cartridges) and blessed with a handful of superb in-house games, but a fraction of the flood available for an Apple II or Atari 800. Second, it created the pressure that eventually cracked the box open. Assembly-language software in ordinary ROM *could* run from the cartridge port (the console checks for it — Ch. 35 lives at that address), and by 1983, with the 4A's installed base soaring, outsiders including Atarisoft — Atari's own software arm, publishing Pac-Man and Donkey Kong for its competitor's machine — walked through that door without asking. TI's response completed the closed-box parable: a 1983 console revision (v2.2) added a check intended to *lock out* unlicensed ROM-only cartridges. The community has never forgotten it, and the lockout's failure — third parties adapted within months, and the bad press outlived the ban — became the platform's founding lesson in openness, learned the hard way by its maker.

For this book, though, GROM and GPL are not villains; they are subject matter. The closed box shaped the machine's entire software culture — it is *why* the OS is an interpreter, *why* cartridges have two different native formats, *why* the community's first great hardware hack (the GRAM Kracker, §1.8) was a device for writing to a memory you were never meant to write. By Chapter 27 you will be building GROM cartridges yourself, with better tools than TI's own engineers had. The moat is now a swimming pool.

> **Field Notes — Decoding a Cartridge PCB.** Get in the habit, from this first chapter, of reading hardware the way you will learn to read hex dumps. Open a photograph of a TI Command Module circuit board — community archives are full of them — and it will tell you its architecture at a glance. A first-party cartridge from 1981 typically shows one to three fat 16-pin chips marked **TMC0430**-something: those are GROMs, 6 K bytes each, and their presence means GPL software enumerated by the console menu. A late-era or third-party board instead shows a standard EPROM/ROM footprint (24/28 pins) wired to the edge connector's address lines: raw TMS9900 code at `>6000`, no GROM, no TI toll — the Atarisoft pattern. Boards with *both* are the hybrids Chapter 29 teaches (GPL shell, assembly core), and a very few carry RAM as well (the Mini Memory, Ch. 6, is the famous one). One glance at chip count and package types, and you know which half of this book wrote the software inside. By the end of Part VI you will be able to predict the cartridge's *header contents* from the photo, too.
## 1.5 The 99/4A (June 1981): The Machine, Finally

TI did not abandon the home computer after the 99/4's cold reception; it fixed it. The revision arrived in June 1981 as the TI-99/4**A**, at **$525** — less than half the 99/4's price, because the monitor bundle was gone (the FCC's rules had eased, and an RF modulator for an ordinary television was now a small accessory rather than a regulatory impossibility).

Two changes defined the A. The first anyone could see: a real keyboard — 48 full-stroke typewriter keys, a proper QWERTY layout with a spacebar, shift keys, and a **FCTN** key whose combinations you will come to know intimately (Ch. 21). The machine still had no true lowercase; pressing the new Alpha Lock off got you "small capitals," a shrunken uppercase alphabet that let text *look* mixed-case at 32 columns. It fooled nobody and shipped anyway, and in Chapter 13 you will learn to replace it in an afternoon.

The second change hid in the video chip. The 4A carried the TMS9918**A**, whose suffix mattered as much as the console's: the revised VDP added **Graphics II mode** — a full-screen bitmap mode, 256×192 pixels with color rules all its own — and support for the cheap standard DRAMs of the moment. Chapter 15 is built entirely on that suffix. (It also created the platform's first compatibility schism: bitmap software simply cannot run on an original 99/4, a fact 1982's magazines had to keep explaining.)

Under the lid, the architecture was otherwise the 99/4's: same 9900 behind the same 8-bit multiplexer, same 256-byte scratchpad, same 16 K of VDP-private RAM doing double duty as the *only* sizable read/write memory until you bought the 32 K expansion, same GPL interpreter running the show. But the price was right, the keyboard was real, the chip shortage of strangeness was over — and the timing appeared perfect. The American home computer market was about to explode. TI had a credible machine, a factory that could make millions, a war chest, a famous brand, and a spokesman with the most trusted face on television.

It also had, arriving at almost the same moment, Jack Tramiel.

> **Sidebar — "How Do You Sell a Computer with Bill Cosby?"** TI's answer to the home computer's marketing problem — what *is* this thing, and why does a family need one? — was to buy the most reassuring celebrity in America. Bill Cosby became the TI Home Computer's television face in 1982 on a contract reported in the seven figures per year, and the campaign is a period time capsule: Cosby in a sweater, grinning beside the silver console, promising that this computer *speaks*, teaches spelling, and won't intimidate anyone. Commodore countered with William Shatner asking, over a VIC-20, "Why buy just a video game?" — and the two campaigns frame the era's real battle, which was never about specifications. It was about convincing normal households that a computer belonged next to the television, at a price that made saying yes painless. The ads are easy to find online and worth five minutes of your time as primary sources, though a modern viewer watches the Cosby spots through the lens of his later disgrace; they have become uncomfortable artifacts twice over — a fallen pitchman, selling a doomed machine, brilliantly.

## 1.6 The Home Computer War (1981–1983)

Jack Tramiel, the Auschwitz survivor who built Commodore from a Bronx typewriter-repair shop into a calculator company and then a computer company, had been on the losing side of TI's calculator war in the mid-1970s, when TI's vertical integration undercut Commodore's margins to the bone. He drew two conclusions and repeated them for the rest of his life: *own your own silicon* (Commodore bought MOS Technology, the 6502 people, in 1976) and — his actual, much-quoted words — **"Business is war."** When TI entered his new market, Tramiel was not planning to compete with it. He was planning revenge.

The armies were mismatched in an instructive way. Commodore's VIC-20 (1981, **$299**) was, on paper, a lesser machine than the 4A: an 8-bit 6502, 5 K of RAM, blocky 22-column video, no sprites, no speech. But every important chip in it came off Commodore's own MOS lines at cost, the board was ruthlessly simple, and its maker was privately willing to sell it for whatever number beat yours. The 4A was the *expensive* kind of vertically integrated: TI fabbed its own silicon too, but the design carried the multiplexer bodge, the GROM subsystem, the elaborate power supply — a bill of materials with 1979's decisions soldered into it. TI's strategists, veterans of calculators, believed the experience curve would save them as it always had: cut price, win volume, ride costs down, cash the software-and-peripherals annuity. That last clause was the plan's load-bearing wall — the console could be a loss leader because a family that owned one would buy cartridges (high-margin, GROM-gated, §1.4) and eventually the Peripheral Expansion Box with its cards. Razor and blades.

The war's price path, reconstructed from period advertising and reporting, reads like an altitude log of a dive: the 4A at $525 (mid-1981), around $299 by spring 1982; a **$100 rebate** in the fall of 1982 that made national news and put the machine near $199; matching cuts from Commodore on the VIC and, ominously, on the new Commodore 64; the 4A at roughly $150 by early 1983; and by that summer, after a further round, a 16-bit computer with hardware sprites and an optional voice selling for **$99** — frequently less after retail games — while Commodore pounded every price TI set within weeks, sometimes days. Somewhere on the way down, TI passed the line where each console sold *lost* money outright, on the theory the software annuity would repay it.

It did not. The blades never sold like razors-and-blades requires — the closed box of §1.4 had kept the library thin, the third parties resentful, and the must-have titles few; the Peripheral Expansion system was priced for the machine's original $525 dignity, not its $99 reality; and Commodore, whose costs were genuinely lower and whose founder enjoyed this, would not stop. Meanwhile 1983 was the year the whole overbuilt home computer market cracked: Atari hemorrhaging, Mattel and Coleco stumbling through their own disasters, Timex quitting, Osborne collapsing — the shakeout was general, and TI was its largest single casualty. On June 10, 1983, TI warned Wall Street that the home computer division's losses for the *quarter* would be around $100 million (the reported figure came in near $119 million); the stock lost roughly a third of its value in the days that followed. The second half of the year was a corporate deathwatch conducted in public. The 99/2 — a $99 black-and-white baby brother announced at January's CES to fight Sinclair — was cancelled when the 4A itself hit $99 and made it pointless. Lubbock's lines kept running into a warehouse. Everyone waited for the earnings release that would say the quiet part.

## 1.7 Black Friday: October 28, 1983

The announcement, when it came with the third-quarter results on the last Friday of October 1983, was brief and total: Texas Instruments would exit the home computer business. The quarter absorbed a write-off reported at roughly **$330 million** pre-tax to cover the withdrawal — inventory, commitments, the works — and 1983 as a whole would close as the first losing year in the company's history, with a reported net loss near $145 million. Wall Street, which had watched the June bloodletting, mostly exhaled; TI stock *rose* on the news that the bleeding would stop. In Lubbock, and in the community, the day acquired the only name it has ever had since: **Black Friday**.

The details around the edges tell you what kind of ending it was. The **99/8** — the genuinely ambitious successor developed under the codename *Armadillo*, with 64 K, an improved CPU, and Pascal in ROM — was cancelled essentially on the loading dock; production-intent prototypes survive in collections and get trotted out at faires like relics of a parallel universe (Ch. 44 pays respects). Third-party developers learned from the newspaper. TI committed to honoring warranties and kept selling and supporting through 1984 as the pipeline drained, and the final consoles — the cost-reduced beige "QI" units — trickled out as the Lubbock consumer operation wound down around them.

And then came the strangest Christmas in computer retail. A discontinued machine has one virtue no living product can match: a price with no future to protect. Retailers cleared 4A consoles at $49.95 — sometimes less — and America, which had spent two years being told by Bill Cosby that this was a real computer, decided that at fifty dollars he was right. Consoles sold out across the country; by several accounts TI moved more machines in the months *after* the exit than in whole years before it. When the dust settled, the commonly cited total stood near **2.8 million consoles** — an installed base larger than most *successful* platforms of the era, owned disproportionately by families who had paid almost nothing and had nothing invested in the machine's corporate fate.

Which is precisely why §1.8 exists. Orphan platforms usually die of loneliness. This one was orphaned into a crowd.

## 1.8 Life After Death (1984–2000): The Orphans Organize

The pattern for a dead platform was well established even in 1984: the magazines fold, the user groups thin out, the software dries up, and within three years the machines are landfill with a rumor of nostalgia. The TI-99/4A broke the pattern so thoroughly that its community coined a proud name for itself out of the insult — the **orphans** — and treated Black Friday not as an ending but as a change of management.

The infrastructure converted first. *99'er Home Computer Magazine*, the glossy that had served the platform since before the 4A, dropped the "99" and became *Home Computer Magazine* within months of the exit, hedging toward other platforms before fading mid-decade — a corporate death of its own. What replaced it was better suited to the new era: in February 1984, a husband-and-wife newsroom in Texas — John Koloen and Laura Burns — launched a plain-paper monthly called *Home Computer Compendium*, soon renamed **MICROpendium**, dedicated to the orphaned machine on the theory that two million-plus owners still needed news, reviews, and type-in programs. They were right for *fifteen years*; MICROpendium published until 1999, and its back issues are this book's single best window into what the community knew and when (App. N). Beneath the magazines ran hundreds of local user groups — meeting in libraries and church basements from Boston to Brisbane — swapping newsletters and disks of *fairware*: software distributed freely with an honor-system request to pay the author, a distribution culture the orphans refined years before "shareware" was a household word. The Funnelweb environment out of Australia became fairware's cathedral — a full disk-based working environment polished release after release by the McGovern family and shipped around the world one 90 K floppy at a time.

The hardware story is even more remarkable, because it required actual factories. With TI gone, third parties that had once fought the closed box now sustained the platform: **CorComp** and **Myarc** shipped disk controllers, memory cards, and speed-ups that outran TI's own. **Millers Graphics**, a small California outfit with a newsletter (*The Smart Programmer*) that read like a systems-programming seminar, shipped the era's two great acts of intellectual liberation: *Explorer*, a software microscope that let owners single-step the entire machine and watch the GPL interpreter breathe (Ch. 22 borrows its trick), and the **GRAM Kracker** — a device that mapped *writable* memory into GROM space, letting mortals finally run, modify, and write the console's secret language themselves. It is not too much to say that Part VI of this book descends directly from that gadget. On the printed page, the German engineer Heiner Martin published *TI Intern*, a book-length disassembly and commentary on the console's ROM and GROMs that turned folklore into reference; it has never really left the community's desk, and it will not leave ours (first invoked in earnest in Ch. 22). The decade's crescendo came in 1987, when Myarc shipped the **Geneve 9640** — an entire successor computer on a card for the Peripheral Expansion Box, built around the improved TMS9995, running its own MDOS and better video: the 99/8's ghost, delivered by the aftermarket (Ch. 44). And through all of it ran the **faires** — annual community conventions of which Chicago's became the flagship — where new hardware debuted, authors met their users, and the platform proved yearly that it still had a pulse.

> **Sidebar — The First Chicago Faire.** The timing is the whole story: the Chicago-area TI users group held its first TI-99/4A faire in **November 1983** — within weeks of Black Friday. Picture the room: a suburban hall full of vendors whose supplier had just publicly abandoned them, hobbyists whose machine was a national punchline, and tables of software and peripherals selling briskly anyway. It could have been a wake. The community chose, apparently without much debate, to make it an annual general meeting instead — and kept choosing that, every autumn, through the fairware years, the Geneve years, the lean late 1990s, and into the internet era. The Chicago International TI-99/4A Faire has now convened for more than four decades, which makes it one of the longest-running platform gatherings in computing. When Chapter 45 talks about where you will show *your* work, this is the room it means.

## 1.9 The Machine Today: Why This Book Is Possible

Somewhere in the 2000s, the community's center of gravity shifted from survival to renaissance, and the platform you will actually use in this book took shape. It rests on pillars worth naming now, because Chapter 3 installs them.

**Emulation matured into instrumentation.** *Classic99* (Mike Brent — "Tursi" to the community) turned the Windows PC into a 4A with a debugger TI's own engineers would have envied — and, through an unusual arrangement, ships with a library of original TI software included under license, a community treasure. *js99er.net* (Rasmus Moustgaard) put a full 4A, expansion system and all, in the browser — zero installation, perfect for a fast start. *MAME* contributes the cycle-obsessive reference implementation consulted when a measurement needs a referee. And emulation matured in one further sense that this book is built on: writing a 4A from scratch became something a dedicated project can *do*, and prove correct against those elders. The **libre99 project** — a pure-Rust console that runs the genuine TI firmware on faithfully modeled chips, with its own assembler, GPL tools, and a period software library aboard — is where this book lives: the manuscript is written inside the emulator's repository, every lab runs on it first, and Chapter 3 makes it your daily machine (with the three elders on the reference shelf beside it).

**New hardware ended the old scarcities.** The **F18A** (Matthew Hägerty) is an FPGA drop-in replacement for the 9918A that adds crisp VGA output and capabilities the original never had — including, astonishingly, a programmable GPU core inside the video chip (Chs. 18, 34, 44). The **FinalGROM 99** is an SD-card cartridge that loads ROM *and* GROM images on real hardware in seconds, making the physical console a first-class development target again (Ch. 27 ships to it). The **TIPI** grafts a Raspberry Pi onto the console as a peripheral, giving a 1981 machine a filesystem the size of an SD card and — through its extension protocol — a path to TCP/IP and the web (Ch. 34 does exactly that). Compact-flash devices (nanoPEB/CF7-class) and modern **SAMS** memory cards fill out a catalog in which, for the first time since 1983, everything the platform ever needed is *in production*.

**And the toolchain became modern.** The community's xdt99 suite — a cross-assembler for 9900 (*xas99*), an assembler for GPL (*xga99*), disk tools (*xdm99*) — proved the pattern: write code in today's editor, build in milliseconds, test in an emulator with source-level comfort, then ship the identical bytes to 1981 silicon. This book's own toolchain follows that pattern from inside the emulator project itself: **libre99asm** assembles Editor/Assembler-style source straight into bootable cartridges, **libre99gpl** does the same for the console's other language (Part VI), and because the emulator's core is a library, the book can hand you the whole console as a scriptable lab instrument. The project has already used these tools to ship a complete original game and — remarkably — a clean-room rewrite of the console's own firmware (Ch. 28 tells that story). New software of genuinely commercial scope ships across the community every year — polished cartridge games with boxes and manuals, ports that were "impossible," system software TI never imagined — much of it debuted at the faires and in the annual development contests. That standard — *shippable, boxed, commercial-grade* — is this book's graduation bar, and the case studies of Part IX are built to meet it.

The machine died in 1983. The platform never did. Let's learn to program it.

> **Myths and Misconceptions.** History chapters get their own pitfalls box. Handle these with care when you meet them in forums and old articles:
>
> - **"It was 16-bit, so it was fast."** The CPU was 16-bit; the *system* mostly wasn't (§1.3). Naive code runs slower than on good 8-bit rivals; well-placed code flies. This entire book lives in that gap.
> - **"TI BASIC was slow because the machine was slow."** No — it was slow because it is an interpreter running *on* an interpreter, with its program text behind the video chip (Ch. 28 shows the receipts).
> - **"TI lost because the computer was bad."** The 4A was competitive hardware sold under a business model that starved its own software library, against an opponent with structurally lower costs. The autopsy says strategy, not silicon.
> - **"Black Friday killed the platform."** It killed the *product*. The platform's most productive decades came after (§1.8–1.9).
> - **Folklore inflation.** Unit counts, loss figures, and who-said-what vary across retellings; this book hedges figures deliberately (`_ledger.md` tracks the forms we assert) and prefers primary sources — a habit worth copying.

## Summary

- The 4A merged two TI bloodlines: consumer silicon (calculators, Speak & Spell/LPC speech) and the TI-990 minicomputer line, whose single-chip TMS9900 (1976) was an early 16-bit micro with registers-in-memory workspaces.
- The cancelled 8-bit-bus TMS9985 forced the full 9900 onto an 8-bit multiplexed board: everything waits except 256 bytes of true-16-bit **scratchpad** — the book's central performance fact.
- GROM (TI-only serial-access ROM) + GPL (interpreted bytecode OS/software) implemented a closed cartridge market; it thinned the library, provoked the 1983 v2.2 lockout, and defines Part VI's subject matter.
- 99/4: June 1979, $1,150 with forced monitor, chiclet keys — a flop. 99/4A: June 1981, $525, real keyboard, 9918A adds bitmap mode.
- Price war vs. Tramiel's Commodore: $525→$99 (mid-1983)→$49.95 clearance; consoles sold below cost against a software annuity the closed box had already starved; reported Q2-1983 loss ≈$119 M.
- **Black Friday = Friday, Oct 28, 1983**: exit + reported ≈$330 M write-off; 99/8 cancelled; ~2.8 M total consoles, many sold *after* death in the strange $49.95 Christmas.
- Aftermath: orphan community — user groups, fairware, MICROpendium (1984–99), Millers Graphics (Explorer, GRAM Kracker), CorComp/Myarc, *TI Intern*, Geneve 9640 (1987), Chicago Faire annually since Nov 1983.
- Today's pillars (installed in Ch. 3): the **libre99 project** — the book's home and daily machine (pure-Rust console + `libre99asm`/`libre99gpl` toolchain + shipped original software incl. a clean-room firmware, Ch. 28) — with Classic99 (licensed TI software included), js99er, and MAME on the reference shelf; F18A, FinalGROM 99, TIPI, SAMS; xdt99 as the community toolchain.
