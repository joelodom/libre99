## The Machine That Talked

In 1979, computers did not talk. They beeped, they buzzed, a few could play a crude tune — but a machine that produced recognizable *human speech*, in a home, from a box on a desk, was science fiction. And then Texas Instruments shipped one. Plug the Speech Synthesizer sidecar onto the left side of a TI-99/4A, and the machine spoke: full words, whole sentences, in a slightly robotic but unmistakably *human* voice. Children learning to spell heard the computer say the word. Players of Parsec heard a calm voice warn them, "Aliens approaching." A generation's first experience of a talking computer was a TI.

This was not a gimmick bolted on late. Speech was TI's signature — the thing no competitor had, the reason to choose their machine — and it came from a genuine breakthrough. A year earlier TI had shipped the Speak & Spell, a handheld toy that talked, and to build it they had solved a problem the entire audio industry considered barely tractable: how to store and reproduce human speech in *almost no memory*. Not a recording — a recording of a word is thousands of bytes, and the Speak & Spell had a few hundred to spare per word. Something far cleverer: a mathematical *model* of the human vocal tract, updated a few dozen times a second, that could reconstruct speech from about **twelve hundred bits per second** — a fortieth of what a raw recording needs. That model is **Linear Predictive Coding**, LPC, and it is the intellectual heart of this chapter.

This chapter has an unusual honesty to observe. The project emulator this book is built on models a console *without* the speech sidecar attached — writes to the speech port vanish, reads return open bus — which is a faithful model of the very common configuration of a TI with no Speech Synthesizer plugged in. So the one thing we can *verify* on the bench is **detection**: correctly noticing that no synthesizer is present, which our `spklib` does. Everything else — the protocol, the vocabulary, the LPC theory, the encoding of new speech — this chapter teaches from the datasheets and the historical record, and points you to the emulators (MAME, js99er) that model the synthesizer itself when you want to *hear* it. That division — verify what the machine can prove, describe the rest with sources, and say plainly which is which — is the book's discipline, and speech is where it matters most.

## The Bridge: From Neural TTS Back to a Filter

Modern text-to-speech is a neural network. You send text to a model — in the cloud or, increasingly, on the device — and it returns audio so natural it is often indistinguishable from a recorded human, with intonation, emotion, and a choice of voices. The model has hundreds of millions of parameters and was trained on thousands of hours of speech. It is one of the triumphs of contemporary machine learning, and to a modern programmer "make the computer talk" means "call the TTS API."

The TMS5200 has no network, no model, and about the compute of a pocket calculator. It makes speech from a *source-filter model*: a theory, worked out in the 1960s and 1970s, that human speech is a sound *source* (the buzz of the vocal cords, or the hiss of turbulent air) shaped by a *filter* (the changing resonances of the vocal tract — the tongue, lips, and jaw forming vowels and consonants). Model the source with two numbers (is it voiced or unvoiced, and at what pitch) and the filter with about ten numbers (the resonances), update those numbers forty times a second, and you can *reconstruct* speech — not play it back, reconstruct it — from almost nothing. This is the same insight modern codecs and even neural vocoders build on; the TMS5200 is the source-filter model rendered in silicon in 1979, and understanding it is understanding the foundation the whole field of speech coding stands on. Neural TTS is far better. But the TMS5200 is far more *comprehensible* — you can hold its entire theory in your head — and that is exactly why it is worth studying.

## 20.1 Why a Home Computer Spoke in 1979

The story begins with the Speak & Spell, and with a small team at Texas Instruments. The idea — a handheld that would say a word for a child to spell — came from Paul Breedlove, and the speech technology from a group including Gene Frantz, Richard Wiggins, and Larry Brantingham. Their problem was memory: speech is data-hungry, and the cheap memory of 1978 could hold only seconds of recorded audio. Their solution, adapted from linear-predictive research (the work of Bishnu Atal and others in the preceding decade), was not to store the *sound* but to store the *model* — a compact stream of vocal-tract parameters — and rebuild the sound from it on a custom chip. The Speak & Spell shipped in 1978, talked, sold enormously, and proved that cheap speech synthesis was real.

The TI-99/4A's Speech Synthesizer is that technology, one generation on. Where the Speak & Spell had its vocabulary baked in, the console's synthesizer — built around the **TMS5200** (a close cousin of the TMS5220) — could speak a resident vocabulary from its own ROM *and* accept new speech data streamed from the computer. TI made speech central to the platform's identity: it was cheap (the synthesizer was famously nearly given away), it was everywhere in the marketing, and it appeared in the software that sold the machine. Parsec's spoken warnings, Alpiner's taunts, the educational cartridges that read to children — speech was the TI's answer to the question "why this computer and not the others," and for a while it was a very good answer. No other home computer of 1981 talked out of the box. The TI did, and it defined the machine.

## 20.2 The Hardware and the Protocol

The Speech Synthesizer is a **sidecar** — a wedge-shaped unit that clips onto the console's left expansion port — containing the TMS5200 synthesizer chip and a **speech ROM** holding the resident vocabulary. The console talks to it through two memory-mapped ports: **`>9000`** to read (status and data) and **`>9400`** to write (commands and data). `spklib` names them `SPCHRD` and `SPCHWR` (from `equates.inc`).

The chip is commanded by bytes written to `>9400`. A command byte selects an operation — *reset* the chip, *load an address* into the speech ROM, *read a byte* from it, *speak* a resident phrase, or *Speak External* — and some commands are followed by data. Reading `>9000` returns a **status** byte whose bits report the chip's state: whether it is currently *talking*, whether its input buffer is *low* (running out of data and needs more), and whether the buffer is *empty*. Those status bits are how software paces itself, and they matter most for the streaming mode.

**Speak External** is the interesting one. Instead of speaking a phrase from the resident ROM, the chip can speak LPC data *streamed from the computer* — you issue the Speak External command, then feed the chip a continuous stream of speech-data bytes, and it voices them as they arrive. The chip has a small input **FIFO** (on the order of sixteen bytes), and the streaming discipline is a producer-consumer loop: watch the "buffer low" status bit, and whenever it says the FIFO is getting empty, push more bytes. The timing discipline is real and it intersects Chapter 17's frame budget directly — you must feed the FIFO often enough that it never starves (which would break the speech with a gap or a click) but you cannot spend so long feeding it that you starve the *frame* (dropping the game's frame rate while it talks). Speaking *and* running a game means interleaving FIFO-feeding with everything else, a few bytes at a time, every frame — the integration problem of §20.7.

> **Verification honesty (R-12).** Our project emulator models a console with **no speech sidecar attached**: writes to `>9400` are discarded and reads of `>9000` return open bus (`>00`), both confirmed on the bench. This is a faithful model of a bare console — the majority configuration — but it means the bench cannot *voice* speech or exercise the FIFO handshake. What it can prove is **detection**, and that is exactly what `spklib`'s `SPKDET` verifies: it runs the datasheet's read-signature protocol and, finding open bus instead of the speech ROM's signature, returns "absent" (`>0000`) — the correct verdict for this console, and the green light of the lab's self-test. The command bytes `spklib` issues to speak are datasheet-derived (App. F) and correct, but this console cannot answer them; to *hear* speech, run the same code under MAME or js99er, which emulate the TMS5220. Throughout this chapter, protocol and vocabulary specifics are datasheet-and-record, not bench-measured, and are flagged where it matters.

## 20.3 The Resident Vocabulary

The speech ROM in the sidecar holds a **resident vocabulary** — a few hundred words and phrases, baked in, that any program can speak without providing a byte of speech data of its own. The set is the vocabulary of the console's own prompts and the common words a program might need: the digits and numbers, letters, "READY," "ERROR," colours, directions, "THAT IS INCORRECT," "TRY AGAIN," and a few dozen more. To speak one, you command the chip to *speak resident phrase N*, where N addresses the phrase in the ROM, and the chip voices it.

Sentences are made by **chaining**: speaking a sequence of resident phrases back to back. "THAT IS A NICE TRY" is not one stored phrase but four or five — "THAT," "IS," "A," "NICE," "TRY" — each triggered in turn, the chip voicing them in sequence to build a sentence the ROM never stored whole. This is why TI speech has its characteristic cadence: it is words concatenated, each with its own recorded intonation, so the *rhythm* between them is even and slightly mechanical — the sentence is assembled, not spoken. The art of using the resident vocabulary is choosing phrasings your available words can build, and ordering them so the concatenation sounds as natural as the fixed word-boundaries allow. It is a constrained-writing exercise: say what you mean using only the several hundred things the ROM can say, and the taunts of Alpiner and the warnings of Parsec are exactly that exercise, done well.

## 20.4 Linear Predictive Coding, Honestly

Here is the theory, and it is beautiful enough to be worth the space. Ask how human speech is produced. Two things happen. A **source** makes a raw sound: for *voiced* sounds (vowels, "m," "z") the vocal cords vibrate, producing a buzz at a *pitch*; for *unvoiced* sounds ("s," "f," "t") air rushes turbulently, producing a hiss. Then that raw source is shaped by a **filter**: the vocal tract — the throat, mouth, tongue position, lip rounding — has resonances that emphasize some frequencies and suppress others, and it is those resonances, changing as you move your mouth, that turn a featureless buzz into "ah" or "ee" or "oh." Source and filter: a sound, and a shape imposed on it.

Linear Predictive Coding is the mathematical version of exactly this. It models the vocal-tract filter as a small set of numbers — **reflection coefficients**, K1 through K10 on the TMS5200, ten of them — that describe the filter's shape, and it models the source with two numbers: an **energy** (how loud) and a **pitch** (the buzz frequency, or a special value meaning "unvoiced — use the hiss instead"). Twelve-odd numbers describe one instant of speech. And because the mouth moves slowly compared to the sound it makes, you do not need a new set every sample — you need one every **frame**, about every 25 milliseconds, roughly forty times a second, and you **interpolate** between frames so the filter glides smoothly rather than jumping. Forty frames a second, a dozen numbers a frame, each number just a few bits — and the arithmetic comes out to around **1,200 bits per second**, a fortieth of the ~50,000-plus bits a second raw audio would need.

Why does so little suffice? Because speech is *predictable* in a precise sense — the "linear predictive" of the name. Each sample of a speech waveform is largely predictable from the samples just before it (the vocal tract is a resonant system with memory), so instead of storing every sample you store the small *prediction filter* (the K coefficients) and the *residual* the filter cannot predict (the source energy and pitch), and the filter regenerates the rest. It is compression by *modeling* — store the rules that generate the data, not the data — and it works because a human vocal tract really is a low-order resonant filter, so ten coefficients really do capture it. This is the same idea, in embryo, behind every modern speech codec and behind the "vocoder" stage of neural TTS. Understanding LPC is understanding the foundational trick of speech compression, and the TMS5200 lets you understand it completely, because its whole model is a dozen numbers you can name.

## 20.5 Making New Speech in 2026

Speaking the resident vocabulary is triggering stored phrases; speaking *anything else* means providing your own LPC data, and producing it is an encoding problem you solve on your PC. The workflow: record the phrase you want — cleanly, mono, a steady voice at a consistent pitch, because the encoder works best on clean input — and run it through an **LPC encoder** that analyzes the recording frame by frame, extracts the energy, pitch, and ten K coefficients for each 25-millisecond frame, and emits the packed byte stream the TMS5200 speaks. The community tools for this are well developed — **BlueWizard** (a Mac app) and **python_wizard** (a Python port of the classic BlueWizard algorithm) are the common ones — and they descend directly from TI's own 1970s encoding methods. The output is a blob of speech data (a `.sph` or raw LPC stream) that you build into your program's ROM and stream to the chip with Speak External.

The craft is in the details. The encoder makes choices — how to quantize each coefficient, whether a frame is voiced or unvoiced, how to set the pitch — and its automatic choices are usually good but not always; hand-tuning the frames (nudging a pitch, fixing a misclassified voiced/unvoiced decision, trimming silence) is how you turn a merely-intelligible phrase into a clear one. It is closer to audio editing than to programming. (These encoders run on your PC and need Python or a Mac app; the PC workstation this book is drafted on has neither the toolchain nor a speech synthesizer to test against, so §20.5's pipeline is described here and run where the tools live — another instance of the book naming exactly where a capability lives when it is not on the bench.) The reward is a machine speaking *your* words, in a voice you encoded — the lab's stretch goal, and a genuinely magical thing to hear a 1979 chip do.

## 20.6 The Allophone Route

There is a second way to make the machine say arbitrary words, and it trades naturalness for freedom. Instead of encoding whole phrases, you string together **allophones** — the basic sound-units of speech, the phonemes and their variants — from a small stored set, to spell out any word phonetically. TI's **Terminal Emulator II** cartridge did exactly this: it accepted *text*, converted it to a phoneme string by rule, and spoke the phonemes, so it could say *anything* you typed, including words no vocabulary contained.

The trade is stark. Resident-vocabulary and encoded speech sound *natural* (they are analyses of real human recordings) but are *canned* — you can only say what you stored. Allophone speech is *unlimited* — it can pronounce any word — but sounds *robotic*, because concatenated allophones lack the natural transitions between sounds that a real utterance has, and the rule-based text-to-phoneme conversion mispronounces irregular spellings (English being English). The choice is a design decision: a game with a fixed set of taunts uses encoded speech and sounds good; a program that must speak arbitrary user text (a terminal, a reader) uses allophones and accepts the robot voice. Knowing both routes — and when unlimited-but-robotic beats natural-but-canned — is the judgment §20.7's integration rests on.

## 20.7 Integration: Speech, Music, and the Frame

Making a game *talk while it plays* is the hard part, because speech, music, and gameplay all want the CPU and all run against Chapter 17's fifty-thousand-cycle frame. The synthesizer helps by doing the actual voicing itself — once you have fed it data, the chip speaks autonomously — but Speak External streaming means you must *keep* feeding it, a few bytes into the FIFO whenever the "buffer low" bit says so, or the speech breaks. So the game loop grows a new tenant: each frame, alongside updating sprites and scrolling and playing music, it checks the speech buffer and tops it up. The bytes-per-frame is small (speech is only ~1,200 bits a second, so a frame's worth is a couple of bytes), but the *checking* must happen reliably every frame, and the FIFO must never starve during a busy frame when the game is also spawning enemies and playing a sound effect. Budgeting for speech is budgeting a small but non-skippable task into an already-full frame.

And the first rule of integration is **detection**. A program that speaks must first find out whether there is anything to speak *through* — because most TI-99/4A consoles had no Speech Synthesizer attached, and commanding a synthesizer that is not there does nothing at best. A well-written program detects the synthesizer at startup and adapts: full spoken feedback if it is present, a graceful fallback (text, or a beep, or simply silence) if it is not, and never a hang or a garble waiting for a chip that will never answer. This is the one piece of the whole chapter our console can prove, and `spklib`'s `SPKDET` proves it: on this no-speech console it correctly returns "absent," the green verdict of the lab. Detection is not a footnote — it is the difference between a program that runs everywhere and a program that runs only on the minority of consoles with the sidecar, and getting it right is the price of using speech at all.

## Summary

The TI-99/4A's Speech Synthesizer — a sidecar built around the **TMS5200**, TI's signature capability and the descendant of the 1978 Speak & Spell — made a home computer talk in 1979 by storing a *model* of speech rather than a recording. The hardware is memory-mapped at **`>9000`** (read status/data) and **`>9400`** (write commands/data): command bytes select reset, address-load, read, speak-resident, or **Speak External** (streaming LPC data through a small FIFO whose "buffer low" status bit paces the feeding). The **resident vocabulary** in the speech ROM is a few hundred words a program speaks by index and *chains* into sentences. The theory is **Linear Predictive Coding**: the source-filter model of speech — a voiced buzz or unvoiced hiss (energy + pitch) shaped by a vocal-tract filter (ten reflection coefficients K1–K10) — updated ~40 frames a second and interpolated, which compresses speech to ~1,200 bits/second by storing the *model* that generates the sound rather than the sound itself. New speech is made on the PC with LPC encoders (BlueWizard/python_wizard) and streamed with Speak External; the **allophone** route (Terminal Emulator II) trades naturalness for unlimited vocabulary. Integration means feeding the FIFO a couple of bytes every frame without starving Chapter 17's frame, and — first of all — **detecting** the synthesizer and degrading gracefully when it is absent, since most consoles had none. That detection is the one piece our project (which models a console *without* the sidecar — writes ignored, `>9000` open bus, both bench-confirmed) can verify, and `spklib`'s `SPKDET` proves it, returning "absent" correctly; the protocol, vocabulary, and LPC are taught here from the datasheets and record, with MAME or js99er named as where to *hear* the chip our bench cannot voice. Part IV is complete: the machine now sings (Ch. 19) and speaks (Ch. 20), the two chips that gave it a voice.
