## Not Everything Is a Disk

Storage taught us the TI's most elaborate I/O — the DSR, the PAB, the filesystem — but the machine has humbler wires out, and they matter both historically and today. A **serial** port (RS-232) carries one bit at a time down a wire to a terminal, a modem, another computer — or, in 2026, to a USB-serial adapter on your laptop, which is how you connect a 1981 console to a modern machine with nothing but a cable. A **parallel** port (PIO) drives a printer, eight bits at a stride, the way computers put ink on paper for decades. And **cassette** — an ordinary audio tape recorder — was, for most TI owners who could not afford the disk system, *the* storage: programs saved as sound, loaded back as sound, at a crawl, with a patience the era demanded. These are the wires out, and this chapter is a tour of the three.

It is also the chapter where this book is most nakedly honest about its ground, because the project **does not emulate any of them**. There is no serial card, no printer port, no cassette in the emulator — a deliberate scope (the cassette deferral is a dated decision in the source, quoted below). So unlike the disk chapters, where a real emulation verified every claim, this chapter is **described from the hardware record and the datasheets**, its code written to assemble and run on the shelf emulators (Classic99, MAME) or real hardware, and its honesty is the point: a good textbook tells you not just how the machine works but *where the ground under a claim comes from*, and here it comes from documents and silicon, not a running emulation. That is a weaker tier than Part VII's disk chapters, and saying so plainly is the R-12 discipline this whole book keeps.

## The Bridge: Serial Never Died

Of everything in this book, the serial port is the technology that most refuses to die. RS-232 — or its logical descendants — is *everywhere* in 2026: every Arduino and microcontroller talks serial over USB; every embedded device has a serial console; a Raspberry Pi's first-boot debugging is serial; industrial equipment, GPS modules, 3D printers, network switches — all serial. The UART (the chip type the TMS9902 is) is one of the most-manufactured circuits in history, still, because "send bytes one bit at a time down a wire with agreed timing" is such a good, simple idea that nothing has replaced it for device-to-device links. When you `screen /dev/ttyUSB0` to talk to a microcontroller, you are speaking the TI's language.

Which makes the bridge here delightfully literal: you can connect a TI-99/4A to your laptop *right now* with a serial cable and a USB adapter, and the two will talk — the 1981 console sending bytes its TMS9902 clocked out, your laptop receiving them on a chip that is the 9902's great-grandchild, both agreeing on 9600 baud and 8 data bits as if forty-five years were nothing. Studying the TI's serial port is studying *the* serial port, the one still on your bench, and the terminal program you write in this chapter (`TERM99`) is the same terminal program you would write for any UART, then or now. Parallel ports and cassette faded; serial is immortal, and learning it here is learning it for good.

## 33.1 The RS-232 Card and the TMS9902

The **RS-232 card** is a Peripheral Expansion Box card (Chapter 30) carrying **two serial ports** and one **parallel (PIO)** port, and at the heart of each serial port is a **TMS9902** — a UART (Universal Asynchronous Receiver/Transmitter), the chip that turns bytes into serial bit-streams and back. The 9902 is, characteristically for this machine, a **CRU device** (Chapter 10): you configure it, transmit a byte, and receive a byte entirely through CRU bits at the card's base (`>1300` for the first serial port, `>1500` for the second, per Chapter 30's map). There are no memory-mapped registers — `LDCR` loads a byte into the transmit register, `STCR` reads the received byte, and single CRU bits carry the status flags (transmit-buffer-empty, receive-buffer-loaded) and configure the format.

There are **two levels** at which you use it. The **DSR level** treats the serial port as a file (Chapter 31): you open a device named `"RS232.BA=9600.DA=8"` — a device name that carries its own configuration (baud 9600, 8 data bits) — and READ and WRITE it with PABs exactly like a disk file, the card's DSR driving the 9902 for you. This is the easy path: serial I/O as file I/O, the whole 9902 hidden behind the PAB. The **register level** is direct 9902 control via the CRU — you configure the chip yourself and poll or interrupt on its flags — which is what you need for **interrupt-driven buffering** (Chapter 30's card-ISR pattern: an incoming byte raises an interrupt, the card's ISR reads it into a ring buffer, so no data is lost while your main program is busy) and for precise control the DSR does not offer. `termlib` (this chapter's code) shows the register level: `TINIT` resets and configures the 9902, `TPUTC` polls transmit-buffer-empty then `LDCR`s a byte, `TGETC` polls receive-buffer-loaded then `STCR`s one — the CRU-serial form. The honest note (R-12) governs it: the project does not emulate the 9902, so `termlib` **assembles** and shows the shape from the datasheet, but runs on Classic99, MAME, or hardware, and the exact CRU bit assignments are the datasheet's (App. G) rather than asserted here at fragile precision.

## 33.2 A Tiny Terminal

The canonical serial program is a **terminal**: read a character from the keyboard, send it out the serial port; read a character from the serial port, show it on the screen — TTY in, TTY out. It is a dozen lines over `termlib` and Chapter 21's keyboard: a loop that checks the keyboard (`KSCAN`) and, on a key, `TPUTC`s it; and checks the serial receive flag and, on a byte, prints it (Chapter 13's `textlib`). That is a working terminal — `TERM99`, the lab — and with **XON/XOFF** flow control (send XOFF when your screen buffer is full, XON when ready, so the sender pauses) it is a *polite* one that will not drop characters.

The payoff is real and modern: point `TERM99` at a serial cable to a **USB-serial adapter** on your laptop, and the TI becomes a terminal to your PC — you can type on the TI and see it on the laptop, and vice versa. And because it is a byte pipe, you can **transfer files with no disk at all**: run a terminal (or a simple send/receive protocol) on both ends and stream a file's bytes over the wire — a program from your laptop into the TI's memory, or a TI file out to your laptop — using nothing but the serial port. For a TI owner without a disk system (many), or a modern hobbyist without a CF7 (many), serial-over-USB is the lifeline that gets code and data on and off the machine. The terminal is the humblest useful program and the most connective: it is how the TI joins the modern world, one byte at a time.

## 33.3 PIO Printing

The RS-232 card's **parallel port** — PIO, Parallel I/O — exists to drive a **printer**, and it is the simplest I/O in the book: eight data bits presented at once with a strobe, and a busy line the printer raises while it digests each byte. At the DSR level it is another named device — open `"PIO"` and WRITE bytes (or lines) to it, and they come out the printer — so printing, like serial and disk, is file I/O: a program that writes a report opens `PIO`, WRITEs its formatted lines, and closes. The **line-printer conventions** are the era's: text flows as lines terminated by carriage return and line feed, the printer advances paper a line at a time, form feed ejects a page, and formatting is done in your text — columns padded with spaces, headers and totals laid out by hand (Chapter 8's arithmetic), page breaks counted and form-fed. There is no page-description language, no fonts beyond the printer's own; you format output for paper the way 1983 did, as monospaced lines you compose byte by byte. It is worth a page because printed output was how work *left* the machine — a program listing, a letter from TI-Writer (Chapter 31's `FORMATDOC`), a table of results — and because "write formatted lines to a device" is a skill that outlived the parallel port (it is how you write to a log, a pipe, a serial console today). The project does not emulate the PIO (R-12), so a printing program assembles and runs against the shelf emulators' printer support or real hardware; the *formatting* — the real content — is yours to compose and verify on paper or a captured file.

## 33.4 Cassette, the People's Storage

Before disks, and for most owners *instead* of disks, there was **cassette** — an ordinary audio tape recorder, plugged into the console, storing programs and data as **sound**. It was the people's storage: a disk system cost more than the console, but a cassette recorder was something families already owned, so the cassette port is how the majority of TI owners actually saved and loaded their work. The mechanism reaches down to Chapter 21's TMS9901: the console drives the recorder through **9901 output lines** — the **motor relays** (CS1/CS2), which start and stop the tape transport under program control, and a **tape-out** line that carries the data as an audio tone — and reads the tape back through a **tape-in** line. The data **encoding** is audio frequency-shift: bits become tones (one frequency for a `0`, another for a `1`), written and read at roughly **1370 baud** — a walking pace — with the console's **cassette DSR** (reached via `GPLLNK`, Chapter 23, since the cassette routines live GPL-side) handling the encoding, the timing, the record/verify handshake, and the "PRESS CASSETTE PLAY" dialogs (Chapter 28's GPL-side services). From assembly you drive it through that DSR — open the cassette device, write or read a block — with the DSR clocking the bits out as tones and the 9901 running the motor.

Here the honest ground is a dated decision in the source itself (R-12). The project **does not emulate the cassette**: the 9901's cassette output bits (CS1/CS2, the audio level) are unwired, and the comment in `cru.rs` records why — *"Decision (Joel, 2026-07-02): cassette stays unemulated for now — the console-ROM rewrite ships interface-correct CS1/CS2 error stubs only, and its tape transport is deferred until these bits (plus the 9901 interval timer and a tape source) exist."* So a cassette program assembles and is described here from the hardware record, but it does not run on the project's emulator; it runs on the shelf emulators that model cassette (with an audio file as the "tape") or on real hardware with a real recorder. The mechanism is genuine and worth knowing — it is how a generation stored its work, and it is the ancestor of every streaming-storage idea since — but the running of it, on this project, awaits the deferred emulation (a clean roadmap item: the cassette P-bits, the 9901 interval timer that paces them, and a tape/audio source).

## 33.5 Modern Tape Tricks

Because cassette storage is *audio*, it does something no other TI storage can: it survives as **sound files**, and that opens tricks the 1980s never imagined. A cassette program can be **mastered as a WAV or FLAC** — the exact tones the console would have written to tape, generated on a modern computer and saved as an audio file — and then *played into a real console* from any audio source: a laptop's headphone jack, or, wonderfully, a **phone**. Load a TI program onto your phone as an audio file, plug the phone's headphone output into a real TI-99/4A's cassette-in, press play on the phone and load on the TI, and the console reads your program off the phone as if it were a 1983 tape — 1370 baud of tones, patiently decoded, a forty-year-old machine loading software from a device that fits in your pocket. It works because the encoding is just audio and audio is forever: a tape is a waveform, a waveform is a file, and a file plays on anything. This is real, community-practiced preservation and fun (tools exist to convert TI cassette programs to and from WAV), and it is the cassette's quiet last laugh — the humblest, slowest storage the machine had is the one that crossed into the streaming age unchanged, because it was streaming audio all along.

## Lab 33 — `TERM99` and a Cassette Round-Trip

The lab is the two ends of "wires out," in `code/ch33/`.

**`TERM99`** — a mini terminal over `termlib` and `textlib`: keyboard-to-serial, serial-to-screen, with **XON/XOFF** flow control so it does not drop characters. Built on `termlib`'s `TINIT`/`TPUTC`/`TGETC`, it is a working terminal — and its real test is connecting a TI (or a shelf emulator) to a modern machine over serial-USB and typing back and forth. It assembles here (`termlib` is clean); it runs on Classic99/MAME or hardware, where the 9902 lives (R-12).

**A cassette round-trip** — save a block of data to "tape" and load it back, and — the modern twist — do the round-trip *through an audio file*: master the block as a WAV, and load it back from that WAV, proving the data survives the trip to sound and back. On the project's emulator this is described, not run (no cassette, R-12); on the shelf emulators or hardware it is a genuine save/load, and mastering to WAV and loading from a phone (§33.5) is the exercise that makes it magical. What you build is small; what it demonstrates — that storage can be a wire, a printer, or a song — is the breadth of "I/O" beyond the disk.

> **Sidebar — 1370 baud and patience.** To load a program from cassette was to *wait*. At roughly 1370 baud, a modest program took minutes — the recorder hissing, the console silent, a screen that said little — and you learned patience because there was no choice. And you learned the **sound**: the leader tone, then the data, a warbling two-frequency screech that every cassette-computer owner of the era can still hear in memory — the sound of a program arriving. You could tell, a little, if a load was going well by its rhythm; a dropout in the audio meant an error and a rewind and a try again, and a bad tape or a dirty head meant the heartbreak of a load that failed at 90 percent after four minutes. It was slow and it was fragile and it was, for millions, *the* experience of loading software — the tones, the wait, the hope. There is a whole generation for whom the sound of loading is that screech, and a whole aesthetic (chiptune, demoscene, "tape loading" visuals) built on its memory. 1370 baud and patience: the price of admission to computing, paid in minutes and static, and remembered with a strange fondness by everyone who paid it.

## Summary

Beyond the disk, the TI has humbler **wires out**. The **RS-232 card** carries two serial ports (each a **TMS9902** UART, a **CRU device** at base `>1300`/`>1500`) and a parallel **PIO** port, usable at the **DSR level** (open `"RS232.BA=9600.DA=8"` or `"PIO"` and READ/WRITE like a file — Chapter 31) or the **register level** (direct 9902 CRU control for interrupt-driven buffering — `termlib`'s `TINIT`/`TPUTC`/`TGETC`, polling transmit-empty and receive-loaded). A **terminal** (`TERM99`, with XON/XOFF) is the canonical serial program, and its modern payoff is real: connect a TI to a laptop over **serial-USB** and move files with no disk at all — because serial never died (every microcontroller still speaks it). **PIO printing** is the simplest I/O — formatted monospaced lines written to `"PIO"`, page breaks counted by hand. And **cassette** was the people's storage: programs saved as **sound** through the 9901's motor relays and a **~1370-baud** frequency-shift audio encoding, driven by the console's GPL-side cassette DSR — slow, fragile, and, for most owners, the only storage there was; its modern trick is that, being audio, a program **masters as a WAV** and loads into a real console from a **phone's headphone jack**, streaming across forty years unchanged. The honest ground (R-12): the project emulates **none** of these — no 9902, no PIO, no cassette (an explicit dated deferral in `cru.rs`) — so this chapter is **described from the hardware record and datasheets**, its code assembling with `libre99asm` but running on the shelf emulators (Classic99, MAME) or real hardware, a weaker verification tier stated plainly. What the wires teach is breadth: I/O is not only the elaborate disk but a byte down a serial wire, eight bits to a printer, and a program sung to tape — the machine's humbler, older, and in serial's case immortal, connections to the world.
