## A Living Platform

Most books about a 1981 computer would end the hardware tour with the peripherals of 1983 — the disk system, the RS-232 card, the speech synthesizer — and call the machine complete, a fixed historical artifact. That would be wrong for the TI-99/4A, because the TI-99/4A is **not finished**. It has one of the most active homebrew hardware communities of any vintage platform, and new boards are still being *designed, manufactured, and sold* today: a memory expansion that turns 32K into more than a megabyte, an enhanced video chip that outclasses the original by every measure and carries its own programmable processor, a Raspberry Pi bridge that gives the console a filesystem, a network, and the open internet. People are writing *new commercial-quality software* for this machine in 2026, and that software targets *this* hardware — so a book whose goal is that you could "recreate any legacy commercial TI-99 program" (and the new ones) must cover the living machine, not just the dead one.

This chapter is that coverage: **SAMS** (the memory), the **F18A** (the video), **TIPI** (the bridge), and the developer conveniences (FinalGROM 99) that make iterating on real hardware fast. And it closes with the doctrine that makes writing for a platform-with-enhancements sane — **stock-first, enhance-if-present** — which is not just good manners but the exact discipline this whole book has practiced: know the baseline machine cold, detect what more is available, and use it if there, degrade gracefully if not. That doctrine is why the project's emulator matters *here* specifically: it models the **stock** console faithfully, which is the baseline every enhancement builds on, so the "stock-first" half of every technique in this chapter is the half the emulator verifies, and the "enhance-if-present" half is what you test on the boards themselves.

## The Bridge: The Retro Hardware Renaissance

There is a broad, thriving movement you may know from other machines: people building **new hardware for old computers**. FPGA recreations of whole systems (the MiSTer project), flash-cartridge replacements for every console, RAM and accelerator boards for Amigas and Apple IIs, Raspberry-Pi bridges that give ancient machines modern storage and networking, enhanced video and sound boards. The retro-computing world is not merely *preserving* old hardware; it is *extending* it, with modern components (FPGAs, ARM SoCs, flash memory) wired into vintage buses, so a 40-year-old computer gains capabilities its designers never imagined — a gigabyte of storage, a network stack, HDMI output — while still running its original software. It is one of the most creative corners of computing: engineering for a fixed, beloved, deeply-understood target, with none of the churn of the modern industry.

The TI-99/4A is a flagship of that movement, and its modern peripherals are exactly this pattern: SAMS is a RAM board, the F18A is an FPGA video upgrade, TIPI is an ARM-SoC (Raspberry Pi) bridge — the same three categories (memory, video, connectivity) the whole retro-hardware renaissance produces. So studying them is studying the renaissance itself, on one machine: how you extend a fixed platform without breaking it, how new software negotiates old and new hardware (the detection doctrine), and how a community becomes, in effect, the hardware company its platform's original vendor stopped being (the sidebar). If you have ever wanted to understand the modern retro-hardware world — or build for it — this chapter is that world in miniature, on a machine you now know to the register.

## 34.1 Why This Chapter Exists

The premise is simple and easy to miss: **the platform's current hardware is part of its software**. When a developer releases a new TI-99/4A game or application today — and they do, regularly, to an active market of enthusiasts — it very often *requires* or *benefits from* the modern boards: it assumes SAMS memory for a world too big for 32K, uses the F18A's enhanced modes for graphics the 9918A cannot manage, or talks to TIPI for online features. The commercial and community software of the *present* is written for the *enhanced* machine, because that is what serious TI users own. So the book's goal — that you could recreate the platform's commercial software, old and new — cannot stop at 1983 hardware: a modern TI title that streams a huge world from SAMS and shows an 80-column F18A display is as much "commercial TI-99 software" as a 1982 cartridge, and recreating it means knowing SAMS and the F18A. This chapter exists because the platform kept living after TI left it, and its living software targets its living hardware. To be complete — to actually reach the goal — the tour must include the machine as it *is*, not only as it *was*.

## 34.2 TIPI: A Raspberry Pi for a 1981 Console

**TIPI** (the TI Peripheral Interface) is a **Raspberry Pi** on a card in the expansion bus — an ARM Linux computer bridging the 1981 console to the modern world. To the TI, TIPI presents itself through the machinery you already know: a **DSR** (Chapter 30) and a **register protocol** — a small set of memory-mapped registers through which the console and the Pi exchange messages (a message-passing mailbox: the TI writes a request, the Pi reads it, acts, and writes a response). On that protocol TIPI builds three gifts. A **mapped filesystem**: DSK-style device names (`TIPI.`, and mapped drives) that resolve to files and directories on the Pi's storage — so the console gets effectively unlimited, modern storage through the same PAB file I/O of Chapter 31, no floppies at all. **Extension calls**: services beyond files — the Pi can reach **TCP sockets** and **web APIs**, so a TI program can, through TIPI, make an HTTP request and get data back, or open a socket to another machine — *the internet, from a 1981 console*, a genuinely astonishing thing (a TI program fetching a web page, a weather feed, a high-score server). And because it is a full Linux computer, TIPI's capabilities grow with software, not silicon.

The programming discipline TIPI demands is **defensive coding for its absence** (§34.6): a TI program that uses TIPI must detect whether TIPI is present — probe its registers or its DSR — and behave sanely if it is not (fall back to disk, disable the online feature, tell the user), because most consoles do not have one. The honest ground (R-12): the project does **not** emulate TIPI, so this is described from the community's documentation and the real board; you develop TIPI software against real hardware or the community's TIPI tooling, and the console-side code — the register protocol, the PAB calls to the mapped filesystem, the extension requests — assembles here but talks to a Pi that this emulator does not provide. What TIPI teaches, beyond itself, is the shape of the future the retro world keeps building: a bridge that lets a fixed old machine ride modern services forever, the internet arriving at a console four decades late and entirely welcome.

## 34.3 SAMS: A Megabyte Through a Keyhole

The stock console has 32K of expansion RAM, and for a big program — a sprawling RPG, a streamed world, a large dataset — it is not enough. **SAMS** (Super AMS) answers with **1 MB or more** of RAM, but the 9900 can only *address* 64K, so SAMS does what every "more memory than the address space" system does: it **pages**. The console's memory map keeps its eight 4 KiB expansion windows (`>2000`, `>3000`, `>A000`…`>F000`), but each window now shows a **4 KiB page** of the big RAM, and which page is a **mapper register**'s choice — so a program reaches a megabyte 4 KiB at a time, remapping windows to bring different pages into view. The mechanism (from the SAMS documentation, wrapped in `samslib`): the mapper's register access is enabled via **CRU base `>1E00`**, and while enabled, a mapper register per window (over the `>4000` area) takes a **page number** that selects which physical 4 KiB page that window shows; `samslib`'s `SAMSMP` maps a page into a window, `SAMSEN`/`SAMSDI` gate the register access.

What SAMS *makes possible* is the payoff. **Far data**: tables, level maps, graphics, and text far larger than 32K, paged into a window as needed — a game's entire world resident in SAMS, streamed 4 KiB at a time into a working window (the lab). **Bank-aware code**: with care, even code can be paged (though the resident-common-segment discipline of Chapter 35's bank-switching applies — the code that *does* the paging must stay resident). The rules are the bank-switching rules generalized: keep your mapper-managing code and your critical data resident (in windows you do not remap, or in the scratchpad), page the bulk, and **detect the size** (probe how much SAMS is present — 1 MB? 4 MB? — by testing which pages exist, so you use what is there). The honest ground (R-12): the project models the **fixed 32K**, not SAMS — no mapper, no `>1E00` — so `samslib` assembles and shows the mapper form, but runs on MAME, js99er, or real SAMS hardware; the stock-32K baseline (which the emulator *does* verify) is what a SAMS program falls back to when SAMS is absent. SAMS is the machine's virtual-memory story: a megabyte seen through a 4 KiB keyhole, paged by a register, and it is how a modern TI program holds a world.

## 34.4 The F18A: A Video Chip With a Processor Inside

The **F18A** is an **FPGA replacement for the TMS9918A** that is a drop-in physically and a revolution functionally. It runs all the stock VDP modes (Part III) unchanged — so existing software just works — and then adds a world on top, unlocked by a special **register sequence** (a "knock" on the VDP registers that the stock chip ignores but the F18A recognizes, revealing its **extended registers**). Unlocked, the F18A offers what the 9918A never could: **more colors** (a full palette, per-tile color, no more two-colors-per-eight-pixels bitmap limit of Chapter 15), **larger and more sprites** without the four-per-line law (Chapter 16), **80-column text** (a genuine 80×24 mode, transforming the machine for productivity and terminals — Chapter 33's `TERM99` on an F18A is a real terminal), **scanline-level effects** (interrupt-like tricks tied to the raster for split screens and smooth scrolling beyond Chapter 17's), and clean modern video output (VGA/HDMI). It is, in one board, the video chip the TI should have grown into.

And then the astonishing part, which this chapter only bows to and Chapter 44 develops: the F18A contains a **GPU** — a **9900-class processor** *inside the video chip*, programmable, with fast access to video memory, able to run code that manipulates the display independently of the main CPU. A TI-99/4A with an F18A is, secretly, a **dual-processor machine** — the 9900 you have programmed all book, and a second 9900-family core in the VDP you can offload graphics work to — which is a genuinely modern architecture (a CPU and a GPU, cooperating) arriving on a 1981 console. Programming that GPU in earnest is a capstone (Chapter 44); here the point is that it *exists*, and that the F18A is not a mere upgrade but a new *architecture* the platform grew. The honest ground (R-12): the project's VDP is a **plain TMS9918A** — no unlock, no extended registers, no GPU — so the F18A is described from its documentation and modeled by the shelf emulators (MAME and js99er implement it), and F18A code runs there or on the real board; the stock 9918A the emulator verifies is exactly the baseline the F18A extends and falls back to. The F18A is the platform's boldest modern leap — a processor in the video chip — and it waits, fully, for Chapter 44.

## 34.5 FinalGROM 99 in Developer Mode

A practical note that changes how you *work*. The **FinalGROM 99** (Chapter 25's GRAM cartridge) is not only a way to run cartridges on real hardware — it is a **development accelerator**. In its developer workflow, you build a cartridge image on your modern machine (Chapter 27's `libre99gpl`, Chapter 35's `libre99asm` cartridge builds), copy it to the FinalGROM's SD card, and run it on the real console in seconds — no EPROM burning, no waiting, just build-copy-run, the tight iteration loop modern developers expect, on 1981 hardware. It closes the gap between the emulator (where you develop and debug fast — the project's whole toolchain) and real hardware (where you confirm and ship): iterate on the emulator with BENCH99 and the desktop app, then push the same image to a FinalGROM and confirm it on the metal, in the same minute. This is how modern TI development actually happens — emulator for speed, FinalGROM for truth — and it is why the book's emulator-first workflow is not a compromise but the front half of a real pipeline, the back half being a flash cart and a real console (R-12: the FinalGROM is real hardware the book names, not an emulator feature).

## 34.6 The Compatibility Doctrine

Writing for a platform with a stock baseline and optional enhancements needs a doctrine, and it is one word longer than "stock-first": **stock-first, enhance-if-present**. Write for the **stock console** as the baseline — the machine every TI owner has, the one the emulator verifies — so your software runs for *everyone*. Then **detect** each enhancement and **use it if present**, degrading gracefully if not: detect SAMS (probe the mapper — does writing a page register change what a window shows?) and use its memory for a bigger world, or run in 32K if absent; detect the F18A (perform the unlock knock and read back a signature the stock chip cannot produce) and use its modes, or run stock graphics if absent; detect TIPI (probe its registers/DSR) and offer online features, or disable them if absent. The **detection recipes** are the practical core — each enhancement has a probe that a stock machine fails harmlessly — and the discipline is to *always have a stock path*, so no user is locked out and the enhanced user gets more.

This is the R-12 discipline of this entire book, stated as a design doctrine: know the baseline exactly (which the emulator gives you — a faithful stock console), detect capabilities rather than assume them, and degrade gracefully. The emulator's role is now clear and honorable: it is the **stock baseline**, the "everyone has this" machine your stock-first path targets and the book verifies against, while the enhancements are detected and exercised on the boards and shelf emulators that model them. A program built this way — stock-first on the emulator, enhance-if-present on the hardware — is the correct modern TI program: universal at the base, richer where the machine allows, and never assuming more than it detects. Consolidated, the detection recipes (App. I gathers them) are the toolkit for writing software that meets the living platform on its own terms.

It is a small taste of the internet-from-the-TI that TIPI makes real — and a demonstration of defensive detection (no TIPI → local scores only). Described here, built against real TIPI hardware (R-12), it is the chapter's most vivid proof that the platform is *living*: a forty-year-old game with an online leaderboard, because the community gave the machine the internet.

> **Sidebar — The community as hardware company.** When Texas Instruments left the home-computer business in 1983 (Chapter 45), the TI-99/4A should, by every rule of the industry, have died — no vendor, no new hardware, no future. Instead something rare happened: the *community became the hardware company*. In the decades since, TI enthusiasts — engineers, hobbyists, small one-person outfits — have designed, manufactured, and sold the hardware TI never would: memory expansions (SAMS and its ancestors), the F18A (a from-scratch FPGA video chip, designed by a community member, that outclasses TI's original), TIPI (a Raspberry-Pi bridge giving the machine networking), flash cartridges (FinalGROM), sound cards, and more — a whole catalog of new boards for a "dead" platform, built by the people who loved it. They ran the crowdfunding, the PCB fabrication, the firmware, the support forums; they *are*, functionally, the platform's hardware division, forty years running, unpaid mostly, driven by affection. It is one of the most quietly remarkable stories in computing: a community that refused to let its machine end, and so kept engineering for it, with modern tools, to a standard that shames the original — the users finishing, and surpassing, what the corporation abandoned. Every board in this chapter exists because somebody who loved this machine decided it deserved a future, and built one. The company left; the community stayed, and became the company.

## Summary

The TI-99/4A is a **living platform**, and recreating its software — new as well as old — means knowing its **modern hardware**, because today's TI releases target it. **SAMS** gives the machine **1 MB+ of RAM** through a **4 KiB paging mapper**: the CPU's eight expansion windows each show a 4 KiB *page* of the big RAM, selected by a mapper register (enabled via CRU `>1E00`; `samslib`'s `SAMSMP` maps a page into a window), enabling **far data** — worlds far larger than 32K, streamed through the keyhole — with bank-aware discipline and size detection. The **F18A** is an **FPGA TMS9918A replacement** that runs all stock modes and, after an **unlock sequence**, adds a full palette, more sprites (no four-per-line law), **80-column text**, scanline effects, and modern video — and, astonishingly, contains a **9900-class GPU** inside the video chip, making the console secretly dual-processor (programmed in earnest in Chapter 44). **TIPI** is a **Raspberry-Pi bridge** presenting a DSR and a register protocol that give the console a **mapped filesystem** (unlimited modern storage through the PAB) and **extension calls** to TCP and web APIs — **the internet, from a 1981 console**. The **FinalGROM 99** developer workflow (build-copy-run) makes iterating on real hardware fast, the back half of the emulator-first pipeline. And the **compatibility doctrine** ties it together — **stock-first, enhance-if-present**: target the stock console (which the emulator faithfully models — the baseline this whole book verifies), **detect** each enhancement with a non-destructive probe, and use it if present or degrade gracefully if not, so software is universal at the base and richer where the machine allows. The honest ground (R-12): the project emulates the **stock** console but **none** of these boards — SAMS, the F18A's enhancements, and TIPI are described from the hardware record and modeled by the shelf emulators (MAME, js99er) and real hardware, where the chapter's code runs — and that stock fidelity *is* the doctrine's baseline. Part VII closes here, on the machine as it lives: not the fixed artifact of 1983 but the growing platform of 2026, kept alive and extended by a community that became, when its maker left, the hardware company the machine deserved.
